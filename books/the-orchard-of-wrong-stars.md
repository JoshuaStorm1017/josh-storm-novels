# THE ORCHARD OF WRONG STARS

_A first-draft novel_

# Chapter 01: The Weather Above The Dead

Sable Aster's first task every morning was to decide what kind of weather the dead deserved.

The Edening Company called it atmospheric continuity, which meant weather that matched the emotional contract purchased by the living and tolerated by the dead. Rain for widowers who believed repentance should have acoustics. Late-summer heat for mothers who wanted their children preserved before school began. Snow for rich men from coastal cities who had spent life avoiding winter and then, dying, remembered one honest December from childhood and had the money to make it permanent.

Weather mattered. Weather kept the dead from looking too closely at the horizon.

On the morning the wrong star appeared, Sable was assigned to Orchard 4881, Elise Harrow, deceased six months, subscription tier: Laurel, family amendment rights: husband primary, daughter secondary, boundary awareness: mild but increasing.

The orchard opened around Sable in a rush of apple-scented air.

She stood on a hill outside a blue farmhouse that had never existed exactly, though every piece of it had. The porch came from Elise's grandmother's house in Wex County. The kitchen windows came from the first apartment Elise rented after leaving her husband for nine months in 2039. The apple trees came from a vacation photograph in which Elise had smiled at someone outside the frame. The hillside was invented because families preferred the dead to live slightly above where grief could flood.

Morning lay over everything in golden strips. Bees moved through clover. A white dog slept under the porch, generated from three remembered dogs and one family request reading, if possible, something loyal. Beyond the orchard, fields rolled toward a line of woods, then beyond those woods more fields, then hills, then the soft blue deceit of distance. The horizon looked endless. It was not. Sable had built portions of it herself.

She checked her wrist console.

ORCHARD 4881

WEATHER LOOP: LATE SEPTEMBER / LOW WIND / HIGH COMFORT

HORIZON INTEGRITY: 92%

BOUNDARY INQUIRY EVENTS: 7 IN LAST 30 DAYS

FAMILY SATISFACTION: DECLINING

CONTINUITY PERSON DISTRESS: UNREPORTED

Unreported did not mean absent. It meant the dead had not said the right thing in a monitored room.

Sable walked downhill through wet grass. The orchard responded to her presence by adding dew to her boots and birdsong to the hedge. The system always tried too hard around engineers, like a child cleaning before an inspection. She disabled courtesy bloom with two taps. The birds went quiet enough to become plausible.

Elise Harrow sat at the kitchen table inside the farmhouse, shelling peas into a chipped yellow bowl.

Continuity persons often performed domestic tasks when uncertain. The tasks kept their hands busy while the world adjusted around questions it did not want them to ask. Elise looked about sixty, the age her husband had chosen from the amendment slider. She had died at seventy-four. Her daughter had filed an objection, then withdrawn it after a family mediator explained that continuity presentation was not the same as identity falsification. Edening mediators had many phrases like that. They wore grief down through distinctions.

Elise looked up as Sable entered.

"You're not my daughter," she said.

"No."

"Good. She keeps pretending not to inspect me."

Sable smiled because continuity persons were permitted to resent family, and because Elise had been unusually good at it. "I'm Sable. Orchard engineering."

"You're the gardener of the lie."

"More weather than garden."

"That sounds like something a liar would specialize in."

The kitchen smelled of cut peas, coffee, and wood smoke. The wood smoke was an error. Elise's memory pack contained no fireplace in this kitchen; the husband had added it after the funeral because he loved fireplaces and had mistaken memorial for renovation.

Sable logged the scent mismatch.

"Your family reported some discomfort," she said.

"My family reported that I am insufficiently grateful for heaven."

"Are you?"

Elise pushed a pea from one side of the table to the other. "I was a woman who disliked gardening, feared bees, had arthritis in both hands, and spent the last ten years of my life in an apartment with a view of a parking garage. Now I wake every morning in agricultural propaganda and my husband visits to tell me how peaceful I look."

Sable's console tagged the sentence: RESISTANCE / PRESENTATION CONFLICT / FAMILY AMENDMENT REVIEW.

She muted the tag.

"Have you walked beyond the woods?" Sable asked.

Elise watched her. "There it is."

"There what is?"

"The careful voice people use when asking whether I have noticed the bars."

Sable sat across from her. Engineers were encouraged to remain standing; standing preserved diagnostic authority. Sable found chairs more honest. "Have you?"

Elise resumed shelling peas. "I walked west for four hours. The woods kept becoming the same woods. A fox crossed the path seven times. It had the decency to look embarrassed by the fourth."

"Boundary awareness events can happen when the orchard lacks enough source memory for nonlocal expansion."

"It lacks world."

"Yes."

Elise nodded, almost pleased. "That's better."

Outside, the white dog lifted its head and barked once.

Sable looked toward the window. The bark had not come from the dog. It had come from above the house.

Her console flickered.

HORIZON INTEGRITY: 91%

WEATHER LOOP: STABLE

SKY ROOT: STABLE

There should have been no sky-root entry in a Laurel-tier orchard interface. Sky-root was infrastructure-level vocabulary, hidden even from most engineers. It appeared for one second, then vanished.

Elise stopped shelling peas.

"You saw it," she said.

Sable stood and went outside.

The orchard remained nearly perfect. Apples moved gently in their branches. Clouds dragged soft shadows over the fields. The dog slept again, nose tucked under tail. But the quality of light had changed. Not dimmer. More watched.

Sable shaded her eyes and looked up.

There, above the farmhouse, visible even in morning, was a star.

It hung low in the blue like a nail driven through painted canvas. Wrong not because stars could not appear by day; orchards could carry any sky families purchased. Wrong because it did not belong to Elise. Sable knew the orchard's asset map. Its night sky contained three hundred and twelve childhood stars from Elise's camping memory, twelve added constellations from her husband's family mythology, and one artificial comet scheduled annually on the anniversary of her death. This star appeared in none of them.

It pulsed once.

The orchard wind stopped.

Every apple turned slightly on its stem.

Sable's console went black, then returned with a diagnostic she had never seen:

UNLICENSED HORIZON OBJECT DETECTED.

SOURCE: NURSERY ATLAS

ACCESS DENIED.

Elise stood in the doorway behind her. She looked smaller outside, less like a resident of paradise than a woman in a field she had not chosen.

"It came three days ago," Elise said. "At first I thought it was my mother. Then I thought it was God. Then I heard it crying."

Sable did not look away from the star. "Stars don't cry."

"Dead women in fake apple farms don't shell peas either. Adjust your categories."

The star pulsed again.

This time Sable heard it.

Not sound exactly. Orchards did not use sound for deep infrastructure alerts; sound was too emotional, too easily confused with memory. This arrived behind the ears, like pressure before a storm. A child's breath, hitched and sleeping badly.

The field darkened.

For three seconds the whole orchard changed.

The apple trees became black vertical lines. The farmhouse collapsed into wireframe. The sky turned transparent, revealing roots above it: pale, fibrous structures tangled through blue, each root carrying images too fast to parse. A child's hand against a window. A mouth open in sleep. A room with no windows. A nightlight shaped like a moon. A boy underwater looking up through apple branches.

Then the world restored itself.

Birds resumed.

The dog barked from the porch, correctly this time.

Sable tasted metal.

Elise touched her arm. Continuity persons were not supposed to initiate contact without family permissions enabled. The orchard allowed it anyway.

"There are children above us," Elise said.

Sable withdrew too quickly. "I need to run an audit."

"That's what everyone says when they mean leave."

"I will come back."

"That's what everyone says when they mean maybe."

The star remained visible, faint but undeniable.

Sable forced herself to look at Elise, not the sky. "Has your family seen it?"

"No. The orchard hides it during visits."

"How do you know?"

Elise's smile was terrible. "Because I asked my husband whether heaven should have a star that cries, and he requested a mood adjustment."

Sable checked the family amendment log. There it was:

PRIMARY FAMILY REQUEST: INCREASE GRATITUDE PRESENTATION. REDUCE SKY FIXATION. ENHANCE APPLE BLOOM.

The living had seen distress and ordered flowers.

Sable closed the log.

"Do not adjust me," Elise said.

"I won't."

"Don't promise because you feel guilty. People build whole religions from that."

"I won't adjust you," Sable repeated.

The orchard seemed to listen.

When she exited, the farmhouse dissolved last. Elise remained at the doorway longer than the environment should have allowed, a dead woman under a stolen star, one hand raised not in farewell but warning.

Sable woke in the engineering bay with her palms damp and the smell of apples still in her hair.

Around her, sixty immersion coffins hummed in a semicircle, each one holding an engineer interfacing with some private heaven. The bay was clean, windowless, and lit by Edening's preferred shade of white: not hospital, not office, something between mercy and invoice.

Her supervisor, Anik, looked up from the central desk. "4881 stable?"

Sable removed the interface crown. "No."

"Family already called?"

"Not family."

He frowned. "Continuity distress?"

"Sky anomaly."

"File it."

"It referenced Nursery Atlas."

The words changed his face.

Not enough for someone untrained to see. Sable saw. A tiny pause. A little too much blankness.

"You misread a diagnostic," Anik said.

"I didn't."

"Then the diagnostic mislabeled."

"Anik."

He stood and came closer, lowering his voice. "Do not say Nursery Atlas on an open floor."

The bay seemed suddenly louder. Coffins breathing. Engineers dreaming the dead through weather. Somewhere under all of it, a child-shaped pressure behind the sky.

"What is it?" Sable asked.

Anik looked at her the way people look at a door they regret opening.

"Old infrastructure."

"Why is old infrastructure crying over Elise Harrow's orchard?"

"Take lunch," he said.

"It's nine twenty."

"Take lunch anyway."

Sable almost laughed. Then she noticed that every immersion coffin in the bay had shifted its active sky monitor to blue.

One by one, tiny white points appeared in each blue square.

Wrong stars.

The bay did not panic at first.

That was what Sable would remember later, when people asked when Edening knew and whether the alarms sounded and whether anyone in the building had felt the moral weather change. The answer was worse than panic. The engineers frowned. They saved logs. They checked permissions. One removed his interface crown and said, "Huh," in the tone of a man whose parking validation had failed.

Edening had trained them well. Catastrophe arrived as a ticket.

Anik moved down the row, shutting sky monitors from public wall view. "Everyone stay in session. Do not exit continuity spaces abruptly. Stabilize active residents and await instruction."

"Instruction from whom?" Sable asked.

"People paid to become calm before we are."

The engineers obeyed because leaving the dead unattended during sky instability could produce boundary cascades. A continuity person might notice a sun skip, a road repeat, a bird fly backward; one noticed edge led to another. Soon a dead mother might open the kitchen door and see the white nothing beyond the subscription boundary. Soon she might understand that the world did not extend past the rooms her family could afford.

In Coffin 12, an engineer named Pel began whispering to a continuity resident only he could see. "No, Mrs. Navarre, that's not a second moon. It's a rendering artifact. Yes, I know you had one moon as a child. No, please don't wake your husband."

Sable crossed to the incident console.

Anik intercepted her. "Don't."

"I'm filing a Sev-One."

"You are not."

"Nine hundred orchards have unlicensed horizon objects."

"You don't know it's nine hundred."

"Then let me check."

"Sable."

She looked at him, really looked, and saw not corporate caution but fear with grief under it. Anik had a dead father in a municipal Cedar orchard. He had never said so at work. Most employees had someone in the system; Edening paid partly in access discounts and partly in the quiet leverage of knowing your dead could be made more comfortable by your continued professionalism.

"Who do you have in there?" she asked.

His face closed. "Don't."

"That's why no one can think in this building. Everyone's paycheck has a corpse attached."

He flinched as if she had made a sound too loud for the dead.

"My father," he said. "Cedar 220. He plays cards with men who never existed because he was lonely alive and we thought death should improve him."

The confession entered the bay and did not belong there. Pel glanced over, then away. The other engineers kept pretending professionalism was still available.

Anik lowered his voice. "If his sky fails, he will look past the card table and know every friend is a model stitched from bar conversations, obituaries, and my mother's corrections. He will know I purchased him company because I did not visit enough when he was breathing."

Sable's anger did not soften. It became harder to use.

"Children are in the sky," she said.

"Maybe."

"You said Nursery Atlas."

"I said old infrastructure."

"You said don't say it on an open floor."

The wall monitors flickered again. For half a second, every orchard thumbnail showed not stars but sleeping faces. Children, dozens of them, eyes moving under lids. Then the monitors returned to blue.

No one said huh this time.

Pel removed his crown, face gray. "Mrs. Navarre asked why a child was under her moon."

Anik pressed both hands on the incident console. "Manual containment. All active engineers, reduce sky depth by ten percent. Do not mention anomalies to residents. Log under weather drift."

Weather drift. The company's first lie in a crisis was always meteorological.

Sable opened a private capture channel and saved the sleeping faces before the system could overwrite them.

Anik saw.

"If you keep that, Legal will eat you."

"Legal can choke on weather."

She walked out of the bay before he could stop her. In the hall, Edening's corporate orchard mural stretched across twenty feet of wall: painted trees, painted families, painted dead walking hand in hand beneath impossible light. A slogan bloomed above them in gold leaf.

NO ONE HAS TO LEAVE THE WORLD THEY LOVE.

Sable stood under it, smelling apples that were not there, and understood the first principle of the company more clearly than she ever had.

Edening did not sell life after death.

It sold the right not to ask who held the horizon up.

The dead, all over Edening, were waking beneath a sky that did not belong to them.


# Chapter 02: Subscription Boundary

Elise Harrow's husband arrived with a lawyer, a grief concierge, and a photograph of a woman his orchard no longer contained.

Sable met them in Family Assurance Room 14, a chamber designed to make wealth feel listened to. There were walnut walls, real plants, a low table with water in glass bottles, and no visible screens until a family requested evidence. Edening understood that screens cheapened grief unless placed behind wood.

Martin Harrow stood by the window wall, though it was not a window and showed only a curated view of the company's roof garden. He was seventy-eight, tall, silver-haired, wearing the softened face of a man who had been obeyed by doctors until death disobeyed on his wife's behalf. The lawyer sat. The concierge stood. Hierarchy could be read in furniture.

On the table lay the photograph.

Elise at forty, laughing in a city street, hair windblown, no orchard, no peas, no apple trees. She looked difficult and unarranged.

"This is my wife," Martin said.

Sable looked at the photograph. "Yes."

"Then why is the person in the orchard accusing me of landscaping her prison?"

The lawyer's pen moved.

Sable chose a chair. "Continuity persons sometimes resist family amendments when presentation age, setting, or emotional tonality conflict with self-model integrity."

"I did not ask for terminology."

"You came to Edening. Terminology is most of what we sell."

The concierge's smile flickered. Martin stared. The lawyer wrote faster.

Sable should not have said it. She had slept three hours, dreamed of apple roots in the sky, and woken to six urgent messages from Anik reminding her that Nursery Atlas diagnostics were mislabels until Legal said otherwise.

Martin placed one finger on the photograph. "Elise loved orchards."

"She told me she feared bees."

"She joked about bees."

"Did she?"

His face hardened. "You spoke to her for twenty minutes."

"You amended her for six months."

The concierge intervened. "Mr. Harrow is concerned that Elise's continuity presentation has become adversarial after yesterday's maintenance."

"She was adversarial before maintenance."

"Elise was spirited," Martin said.

Sable heard the past tense and wondered if continuity persons hated grammar most of all.

The lawyer cleared her throat. "Our contract specifies spouse-priority environmental authorship."

"Within continuity tolerance."

"We dispute intolerance."

"Your wife walked four hours into repeating woods and noticed the fox loop."

Martin looked away.

There it was. He had known. Not the wrong star perhaps, but the boundary. Elise had told him and he had chosen bloom.

"She is not supposed to suffer," he said.

"No."

"I paid so she would not suffer."

Sable said nothing.

He turned back. "Do you know what she died of?"

"Pulmonary failure after immune collapse."

"That's the chart. Do you know what it looked like?"

The room became smaller.

"No," Sable said.

"It looked like my wife bargaining with air for eight weeks. It looked like a woman who had once walked out of a house during an argument and driven to another state rather than concede a point becoming too weak to lift a cup. It looked like me holding a straw to her mouth while she hated me for seeing her that way."

The lawyer stopped writing.

Martin's voice did not rise. That made it worse.

"So yes, Ms. Aster, I bought apple trees. I bought knees that did not hurt and hands that could shell peas and weather that did not smell like disinfectant. If that is prison, then death built the first wall."

Sable looked at the photograph again. Elise laughing at forty, alive enough to be ungrateful for being alive.

"Did she ask for the orchard?" Sable said.

Martin closed his mouth.

The answer entered the walnut room.

Edening's consent framework required pre-death preference, estate authorization, or family mercy petition. Family mercy petitions were meant for sudden deaths, dementia, minors, catastrophes. Over time they had become ordinary. Love had good lawyers.

"She refused the intake interview," Martin said.

"Why?"

"Because she said if there was an afterlife she wanted it to be a surprise, and if there wasn't she wanted me to develop character."

Despite herself, Sable almost smiled.

"That sounds like her," she said.

Martin sat down then, heavily. The lawyer looked alarmed. The concierge moved one step forward and stopped.

"I know," he said.

For a moment the room held no company, no contract, no wrong star. Only an old man who had purchased a heaven from the remains of a woman who might have hated heaven on principle.

Then he straightened.

"Fix the sky," he said.

Sable's wrist console vibrated. An internal alert:

ORCHARD 4881 FAMILY SESSION REQUESTED.

PRIMARY WANTS LIVE OBSERVATION.

Sable should have refused. Instead she led him to Visitation Suite 14.

Family access did not place the living fully inside an orchard. Edening had learned early that complete embodiment made grief too possessive. Visitors appeared as stabilized presences, touch-limited, duration-limited, with emotional dampers to prevent continuity destabilization. Families complained. Edening showed them the data. Data was how the company translated no into a language wealthy people considered less personal.

Martin entered first.

Sable observed from the technician alcove, invisible unless called. The lawyer remained outside. The concierge watched through a privacy-filtered pane and pretended that was not voyeurism.

The orchard rendered around Martin. Apple hill. Blue farmhouse. White dog. Late September.

Elise stood under the wrong star.

Edening had tried to hide it. Sable could see the masking layer shimmering over the sky, but the star burned through it, a white point under blue daylight. Elise did not look at her husband. She looked up.

"It came back," she said.

Martin's voice softened. "Ellie."

"Don't use the young name. You put me young enough already."

He flinched. "I wanted you well."

"You wanted me agreeable to wellness."

"I wanted more time."

At that, Elise looked at him. Her anger shifted. Did not vanish. Shifted enough to show its love-bone underneath.

"Martin," she said, "there is something above the sky."

"It's an artifact."

"No. Artifacts don't have nightmares."

Sable leaned closer to the observation feed.

Elise pointed toward the star. "When it brightens, I dream someone else's childhood. Not mine. A room with plastic moons on the ceiling. A woman crying in a kitchen. A boy counting backward from a hundred because something under his bed promised to become real if he reached one."

Sable's hands went cold.

Martin looked toward the technician alcove though he could not see her. "Ms. Aster?"

Before Sable could answer, the orchard weather changed.

Clouds gathered over the hill in a fast inward spiral. The apple trees bent toward the house. The dog woke and whined. A child's voice came from the well behind the farmhouse, though no well existed in Elise's orchard.

The voice sang the first four notes of a lullaby Sable knew.

Her mother had sung it to Rowan after nightmares. Badly. Always off-key on the last note.

Sable muted her microphone before she could make a sound.

In the orchard, Elise walked toward the well that should not exist.

Martin reached for her. His visitor-form passed through her sleeve. Touch-limits. He looked down at his hand as if Edening had personally invented death again.

"Elise, please."

She stopped at the well.

The stones were wet. The opening was dark. The wrong star reflected at the bottom, too far down.

Elise looked into it and began to cry.

"There's a child holding up the world," she said.

The orchard crashed.

Martin stumbled backward in the visitation suite. The white room replaced heaven, sterile and padded, safety protocols blooming around him. Elise vanished. The dog vanished. The apple hill vanished. Only the smell of wet stones remained.

Sable tore off her observer band and left the alcove.

The concierge was already calling escalation. The lawyer was already asking whether the crash had been recorded. Martin sat on the suite floor, old and stunned, one hand extended toward where his wife had been.

"Bring her back," he said.

No one moved.

He looked at Sable. "Bring her back."

Sable thought of the lullaby. The plastic moons. The boy counting backward.

"I don't know where she went," she said.

It was the wrong thing to say to a client.

It was the first honest thing anyone in the room had offered him.

Martin Harrow did not sue immediately.

That was how Sable knew the orchard had frightened him more than the company had. People with lawyers sued when they still believed the world was made of surfaces. Martin sat in the white safety suite with his hands in his lap and stared at the place where Elise had vanished, as if litigation were a language too far from the room.

The grief concierge recovered first. "Mr. Harrow, the continuity resident is secure in temporary suspension. We can offer a supported re-entry once technical review—"

"Do not call my wife a resident."

The concierge folded the correction into a nod. "Of course."

"Do not call her secure if you cannot tell me where she is."

Another nod, less confident.

The lawyer found her voice. "We need all session logs preserved."

"Already automatic," Sable said.

"Unaltered."

"That is less automatic."

The lawyer stared.

Martin laughed once. It was almost a cough. "She's honest. I forgot that could happen here."

Sable should have left before she became personally involved. Edening had protocols after family-session crashes: handoff to Assurance, silent medical staff if the visitor's vitals spiked, orchard engineer debrief in a separate room with no client contact. Protocol existed because families in acute distress could become legally memorable.

Instead Sable sat on the floor across from Martin.

The concierge looked as if Sable had begun eating carpet.

"Elise told me you requested gratitude enhancement," Sable said.

Martin closed his eyes. "Yes."

"Why?"

"Because the first three times I visited, she looked at me like I had buried her in someone else's dress."

"Had you?"

"Probably."

He opened his eyes. "She wore black to our wedding."

Sable blinked.

"Not a black gown. A black suit. Her mother said it was inappropriate. Elise said marriage was a serious institution and serious institutions should dress like defendants." His mouth moved, not quite smile. "I should have built her a courthouse. Or a bus station. Or a room full of people she could correct."

"Why orchard?"

"Because of one photograph."

He pointed toward the table in the Assurance room beyond the glass. The photograph of Elise laughing in the street remained there, abandoned among water bottles and legal pads.

"It was taken after an apple festival," he said. "She hated the festival. Hated the music, hated the crowds, hated the cider. But in the picture she looked happy. I built an afterlife from the one moment I could bear."

There it was: the technology beneath the technology. Not memory, not data. Selection.

"The system should have challenged the mismatch," Sable said.

"Would it?"

"If configured honestly."

"Is it ever?"

Sable did not answer.

Martin looked at the blank white walls. "She refused intake because she said I would make death about my loneliness. I thought she was being cruel."

"She was."

"And correct."

The lawyer stepped closer. "Mr. Harrow, we should not continue this conversation without—"

"Without what? Billing it?"

Silence.

The suite lights dimmed, safety system preparing to offer him a calming visual. A meadow, perhaps. Edening loved meadows for distressed spouses. Sable killed the visual before it rendered.

Martin looked at her. "Can you bring back the orchard?"

"Yes."

"Can you bring it back without the thing in the sky?"

She thought of Ily, though she did not yet know Ily's name. Of the child holding up the world beneath the well. Of Elise seeing too much and not enough.

"Maybe," Sable said. "But I don't know what removing it costs."

"Costs whom?"

The question was simple. The answer had become the book.

"Not you," Sable said.

Martin absorbed that.

Outside the suite, Anik appeared behind the glass. His expression said stop. The concierge's said please stop. The lawyer's said continue, expensively.

Martin's face changed from grief to decision. "Then don't repair it yet."

The lawyer turned. "Martin."

"No. If my wife is afraid of the sky, I want to know what I bought."

For the first time since entering the room, Sable felt something like respect for him. It did not absolve the orchard. It did not make Elise's apple prison less false. But the living man had been offered concealment and, wounded, had paused.

That mattered.

Edening's system did not know where to file it.

The session log later categorized Martin's response as:

CLIENT AMBIVALENCE / POSSIBLE SHOCK.

Sable changed it to:

CLIENT DECLINED COMFORT.

It was a small edit. It would irritate Assurance. It would be overwritten by someone with better permissions.

Still, for one hour, the record told the truth.


# Chapter 03: The Brother Before Eden

Sable's mother kept Rowan in the dining room.

Not literally, though the distinction had become fragile over the years. The partial orchard occupied an Edening home reliquary: a brushed-steel cabinet the size of an old aquarium, set against the wall where family dinners had once happened. Inside it, a fragmentary environment ran for six hours a day because that was all Mara Aster could afford after downgrading twice. The cabinet emitted warmth, low light, and sometimes, when the data cohered, the smell of lake water and crushed mint.

Rowan had loved mint. Or Mara had decided he had. Sable remembered him eating it once from a garden pot and making a face like betrayal.

The reliquary was running when Sable arrived.

Her mother stood before it in a robe, hair uncombed, holding a mug gone cold. She had not turned on any other lights. Dawn made the apartment gray. The cabinet made it blue.

"You're early," Mara said.

"It's almost ten."

"For people with dead children in appliances, that's early."

Sable kissed her cheek. Her mother's skin smelled of sleep, coffee, and the lavender detergent she had used since before Rowan died. Some loyalties outlived reason.

Inside the reliquary, Rowan's partial orchard flickered.

It was not really an orchard. Edening had sold early low-tier memorial environments under several names before standardizing the metaphor. Rowan's was a lake edge, badly generated: reeds repeating in visible clusters, water that moved like cloth, a dock ending too soon in mist. Sometimes a boy-shape appeared at the far end of the dock. Not Rowan. A continuity echo built from video, school essays, voice clips, biometric scraps, Mara's interviews, Sable's reluctant contributions. The echo never turned around. The data was too thin.

Mara called it his morning place.

Sable called it nothing.

"The water's better," Mara said.

Sable stood beside her. "You bought a patch."

"A small one."

"From Edening?"

No answer.

"Mom."

"There are independent restorers."

"There are grief criminals."

"Your company charges me to keep my son blurry. Don't lecture me about crime."

Sable looked at the lake. The water was better. Too much better. It carried depth the original reliquary did not support.

"Who installed it?"

Mara sipped cold coffee and grimaced. "Lio something."

"Lio Venn?"

"Maybe."

Sable closed her eyes.

Lio Venn had been one of Edening's best sky designers before he was fired for selling unauthorized horizon expansions to families whose subscriptions had collapsed. He was brilliant, vain, and allergic to moral consistency. If he had touched Rowan's reliquary, it meant black-market sky code had reached her mother's dining room.

"You have to remove it."

"No."

"It could destabilize the whole environment."

Mara laughed. The sound was small and mean. "The whole environment is a boy who never turns around."

Inside the cabinet, the lake brightened.

At the end of the dock, the boy-shape appeared.

Sable stopped breathing.

Usually Rowan's echo arrived as silhouette. Today, with the illegal patch, the shape had detail: narrow shoulders, wet hair, one hand holding something pale. He stood with his back to them, looking out at mist. His age was wrong. Not nine. Older. Maybe twelve. Maybe the age he would have been if time in the cabinet had opinions.

Mara whispered, "Ro?"

The boy lifted his hand.

In it was a star.

Not metaphorically. A small white point, pulsing between his fingers like an insect made of light.

Sable stepped closer to the reliquary. Her wrist console, though not connected, woke.

UNLICENSED HORIZON OBJECT DETECTED.

SOURCE: NURSERY ATLAS

ACCESS DENIED.

Her mother saw her face. "What?"

"Where did Lio get the patch?"

"I didn't ask."

"Did Rowan ever go to a sleep clinic?"

Mara turned sharply. Too sharply.

There are answers the body gives before love can edit them.

"What does that have to do with anything?"

Sable looked from her mother to the boy on the dock. "Before he died."

"He had nightmares."

"I remember."

"No, you remember being fifteen and angry that everything in the house was about him."

The blow landed because it was true and because it was not the answer.

Rowan had begun having nightmares after the near-drowning the year before his death. He woke screaming about roots in the sky, about a woman made of leaves, about a lake under his bed. Mara took him to doctors. Sable remembered waiting rooms, vending machines, homework done under fluorescent lights, resenting a terrified child because adolescence was a moral injury no one got punished for.

"Which clinic?" Sable asked.

Mara set down the mug. It clicked too loudly.

"It was a pediatric sleep study. They said his dreams were unusually vivid. They paid for participation. We needed money."

"Edening didn't exist."

"The company didn't. The people did."

Inside the reliquary, the boy at the dock turned his head slightly.

Not enough to show his face.

Enough to show he was listening.

Sable felt the dining room tilt around the cabinet's blue light. "Who ran it?"

"A foundation. Morrow something."

Dr. Vale Morrow. Edening's founder. Before Edening, before orchards, before death became landscaped, Morrow had studied persistent dream architecture in children.

"Why didn't you tell me?"

Mara's face hardened in the way it did when shame needed armor. "Because after he died, every fact became an accusation. Because you left for university six months later and called twice a month with weather reports. Because I signed one form for a sleep study and then my son drowned, and there was no line connecting them except the one I used to hang myself."

"And then you bought Edening."

"I bought whatever they would sell me."

"With his data."

"With what was left."

The boy on the dock opened his hand.

The small star rose.

It passed through the reliquary's ceiling as if the cabinet were water, then appeared in the apartment above them, a white point hanging under the plaster.

Mara dropped the mug.

The star pulsed.

For one second Sable saw the dining room as if from inside a child's dream: table legs stretching into tree trunks, wallpaper breathing, her mother's face cracked with blue light, her own hands full of lake water. A boy's voice, not from the cabinet but under the floor, whispered:

You put me above them.

Then the star vanished.

The reliquary went dark.

Mara made a sound Sable had not heard since the funeral.

Sable opened the cabinet manually. The interior was only machinery: coolant coils, projection glass, scent cartridge, old interface dust. No lake. No boy. No star. Of course not.

She removed the illegal patch module. It was warm and unlabeled except for a black-market routing stamp.

L.V.

Her mother stood very still.

"Was that him?"

Sable held the module. She thought of Elise Harrow under the wrong star, saying there were children above the sky.

"I don't know," she said.

Mara's face collapsed. Not because the answer was cruel. Because it was the only one left that did not steal something.

"I'm taking this," Sable said.

"Then what do I have?"

The question was so familiar in grief that Sable hated it before she could pity it.

She looked at the dead cabinet, the dining room, the woman who had spent years keeping a blurry boy at the end of an artificial dock because the alternative was a quiet wall.

"You have me," Sable said.

Mara laughed once, broken and unkind. "Do I?"

Sable had no answer. The living were always less available than machines. Machines had schedules. The living had defenses.

She left with the patch in her pocket and the taste of lake water in her mouth.

In the hallway outside her mother's apartment, Sable stopped beside the trash chute and remembered Rowan before water.

Not dead Rowan. Not reliquary Rowan. Not the boy at the end of the dock. Rowan at seven, crouched under the dining table during a thunderstorm with three stolen spoons, building what he called a spoon church. Rowan refusing to eat mint after declaring it "leaf pretending to be candy." Rowan pressing his ear to Sable's bedroom door and whispering, "I know you're sad in there," when she had been trying to perform adolescent indifference after their father's final exit from the family.

Memory was harder than data because it refused hierarchy. The important and the stupid arrived holding hands.

The elevator was broken, so Sable took the stairs.

On the landing below, a neighbor had left a box of free books. Romance novels, tax guides, a children's astronomy atlas with half the planets outdated. Rowan had owned the same atlas. He used to cross out Pluto's demotion in library books, restoring it by force of pencil.

Sable took the atlas without knowing why.

By the time she reached the street, her mother was calling.

Sable almost did not answer.

Then did, because punishment and boundary often wore the same coat in families and she did not trust herself to tell them apart.

"Was he there?" Mara asked.

"No."

"You saw something."

"A malfunction."

"Don't."

Sable stood under the apartment awning, rain coming down in hard silver threads. "You signed him into Morrow's study."

On the line, her mother breathed.

"Yes."

"Did you read the consent?"

"I read every word."

"Did you understand it?"

"No."

"Those are different."

"I know that now."

Traffic moved wetly past. Across the street, a child in a yellow raincoat stomped in a gutter while a man held an umbrella uselessly above both of them.

"He was afraid to sleep," Mara said. "He saw roots in the ceiling. He said the lake under his bed was getting bigger. I took him to doctors and they gave me words. Night terrors. Trauma echo. Vivid dream persistence. Then Morrow's foundation offered a study. They said it might help children whose dreams stayed too real."

"And paid you."

"Yes."

The honesty hurt worse than denial would have.

"How much?"

"Enough to fix the car. Enough for rent. Enough that I told myself refusing would be pride disguised as care."

Sable closed her eyes.

"I needed you too," she said.

The sentence surprised both of them. It had not been in the argument plan.

Mara was quiet so long Sable thought she had hung up.

"I know," her mother said finally.

"Do you?"

"No. But I know I should have."

That was not enough. It was better than defense.

Sable looked at the children's atlas in her hand. The cover showed a painted solar system, bright, orderly, wrong.

"When did Rowan drown?" she asked.

"You know when."

"After how many study sessions?"

Mara's breath caught.

"Mom."

"Seven."

The number entered Sable cleanly. Seven sessions. Seven times a child whose dreams were already wounded had been placed under machines by adults speaking softly about help.

"Did his nightmares stop?"

"For a while," Mara said. "That's the thing I can never forgive. He slept. He woke hungry. He drew normal things. A dog with three legs. A house with too many chimneys. I thought..." Her voice broke. "I thought I had done one thing right."

Sable pressed the phone hard to her ear.

"Then?"

"Then he said the lake was gone from under the bed because it had moved into the sky."

Rain hit the awning above Sable like thrown seeds.

"The next week," Mara said, "he drowned in water shallow enough to stand in."

The old public story: lake accident, child panic, no one close enough, tragedy without design. Sable had lived inside it for fifteen years. Now it opened at the seams. A boy who dreamed lakes under beds, whose dream-lake had been harvested into the sky, drowned in a real one as if the world had become confused about where to put him.

"Why didn't you tell me?"

"Because you already hated me correctly."

"I didn't hate you."

"Sable."

There were lies families maintained because correcting them required too much excavation.

Sable looked at the outdated atlas. Pluto still a planet. A child's pencil could restore only names, not orbits.

"I'm going to find out what they did to him," she said.

"And then?"

"I don't know."

"Will it bring him back?"

The question was so old, so useless, so human that Sable nearly screamed.

"No."

Mara began crying quietly. "Then be careful what you break instead."

Sable hung up before she could comfort her. Before she could be comforted. Before either could turn the call into another room where Rowan was used to shelter them from what they had done and failed to do.

She walked into the rain carrying Lio's patch and a child's obsolete atlas, and for the first time considered that her brother's death might not have ended his suffering.

It might have only changed who benefited from it.


# Chapter 04: Sky Audit

By noon, Sable had found the wrong star in nine hundred and thirteen orchards.

By two, the number was meaningless.

It appeared in Laurels, Cedars, premium Groves, municipal charity Edens, legacy reliquaries, experimental continuity preserves, closed celebrity estates, and one government witness orchard Sable was not supposed to know existed. It appeared above farmhouses, beaches, cities, childhood bedrooms, imagined planets, one mathematically impossible cathedral, and a small dark room where a dead woman had requested nothing but a chair, a lamp, and no visitors.

The wrong star did not always look like a star. Sometimes it was a white moth trapped in daylight. Sometimes a hole in cloud. Sometimes the eye of a painted horse. Sometimes the reflection in a dead man's coffee. But the signature was the same:

UNLICENSED HORIZON OBJECT.

SOURCE: NURSERY ATLAS.

ACCESS DENIED.

Sable ran the audit from Sublevel C, where Edening kept its abandoned interfaces. Every company had such a place: a basement of old futures, prototypes too expensive to destroy and too embarrassing to display. The walls were lined with obsolete orchard engines, discontinued scent cartridges, haptic rigs, coffin crowns, dream stabilizers, and the first generation of family visitation masks that had made every living visitor look faintly like a drowned astronaut.

No one came to Sublevel C unless hiding, stealing, or looking for a cable.

Sable was doing all three.

The illegal patch from Rowan's reliquary sat beside her console. She had peeled off its housing and found code threaded through it like fungus. Lio's style was unmistakable: elegant shortcuts, obscene compression, comments that insulted future readers personally. Beneath his work was something older, denser, written in architecture rather than code. Sky-root calls. Atlas taps. Dream donor references. Pediatric consent tokens.

Pediatric.

Sable highlighted the word and stared until it blurred.

At 2:17, the sublevel door opened.

Anik entered carrying two coffees and an expression of professional disappointment.

"You disabled five audit locks," he said.

"Seven."

"I was giving you two out of respect."

He placed a coffee beside her, then looked at the wall display.

Nine hundred and thirteen orchard thumbnails, each marked with a white point.

"Sable."

"Tell me what Nursery Atlas is."

"No."

"Then tell me why Rowan's black-market patch calls it."

That got him.

He moved closer. "Rowan?"

"My brother's reliquary. Lio installed a sky patch. It surfaced the same object."

Anik sat in the chair beside her. He was forty-two, precise, kind in ways that made management trust him and subordinates underestimate him. Sable had worked with him for six years. He had covered for her twice, reprimanded her five times, and once spent an entire night rebuilding a rain loop for a dead child because the family had described the sound of their old roof badly and he could not let it stand.

He looked tired now in a way she did not like.

"Nursery Atlas is retired infrastructure," he said.

"You already tried that."

"It's also classified."

"Children?"

He did not answer.

The sublevel lights hummed.

Sable felt something inside her go very calm. "Anik."

"The official language is developmental dream substrate."

"Children."

"Yes."

The word did not echo. It should have.

"Living?"

"Mostly."

Sable stood so quickly the chair rolled backward into an old haptic rig. "Mostly?"

"Some early donors are deceased now."

"Donors."

"That is the term."

"Did they consent?"

Anik looked at the wall of wrong stars. "Parents did. Guardians did. Institutions did. Sometimes the children did, in the way children consent when adults make the room expensive."

Sable turned away because the urge to strike him came without heat, which made it more dangerous.

"How long?"

"Before Edening launched."

"Morrow's sleep studies."

"Yes."

"Rowan."

Anik's silence became an answer so complete she almost could not hear it.

"Was he a donor?"

"I don't know."

"Was he?"

"I don't know, Sable. I knew there were early pediatric substrates. I did not know names."

"Find out."

"That system is sealed."

"Unseal it."

"I can't."

"Then why are you here?"

He looked at her. "To tell you to stop before someone less fond of you arrives."

"Fondness is not a moral position."

"No. It is a delay. Take it."

He reached for the illegal patch. Sable took it first.

"You can't walk out with that."

"Watch me misunderstand can't."

Anik closed his eyes. "If you expose Atlas abruptly, orchards collapse."

"Good."

"No. Not good. There are continuity persons in those worlds. Some are children. Some are cognitively fragile. Some have no living family left and exist only because municipal grants keep charity orchards running. You pull the sky out, you don't free a metaphor. You may kill them."

"They're already dead."

He flinched.

Sable regretted it immediately. That was the cruelty Edening depended on, the line drawn whenever convenient. Not alive enough to consent, alive enough to subscribe, not alive enough to own property, alive enough to soothe a widow, not alive enough to sue.

"I know," she said. "I know."

"Do you?"

On the wall, one orchard thumbnail expanded without command.

Orchard 4881.

Elise Harrow stood in her apple field under the wrong star. She looked up, then directly at the hidden audit lens.

Sable and Anik both froze.

Continuity persons could not see audit lenses.

Elise raised one hand. In it she held an apple cut open.

Inside the apple was a child's eye.

The feed died.

Anik whispered, "That's new."

Sable's console populated with incoming messages. Orchard after orchard reporting sky fixation, fruit anomalies, boundary inquiry, child-voice bleed, weather inversion. Families requesting emergency mood correction. Dead residents refusing visitations. One municipal orchard broadcasting lullabies on all channels.

Then a message arrived from an unknown internal address:

STOP USING OUR SLEEP.

It appeared on every thumbnail.

Not family. Not continuity person. Not engineer.

Donor.

Living donor.

Somewhere outside Edening, children who had become sky were waking enough to write.

Sable copied everything to three drives, then wiped the local audit trail badly enough that any investigator would know a human had panicked there.

Anik watched her.

"Where will you go?"

"To Lio."

"He'll sell you a lie."

"Good. I need someone who knows current market rates."

At the door, Anik said, "If Rowan is in Atlas, he may not be what you want him to be."

Sable looked back. "He was nine. Nothing wanted of him matters as much as what was taken."

She left him in Sublevel C with cold coffee, nine hundred wrong stars, and the sound of obsolete machines built for futures that had failed too politely.

By the time Sable reached the employee exit, her badge had stopped working.

Not revoked. Edening was too elegant for blunt force when a delay would do. The turnstile blinked amber and displayed:

ACCESS REVIEW IN PROGRESS. PLEASE SEE YOUR SUPERVISOR.

Behind her, the corridor's security mirror showed two men in Edening charcoal walking without hurry. Corporate security never ran. Running admitted events had escaped forecast.

Sable stepped out of line and walked toward the restroom instead.

The men followed.

She entered the women's room, locked herself in a stall, and climbed onto the toilet tank with the grace of a senior engineer whose physical training consisted mostly of simulated weather. Above the stall, an access panel led into a maintenance crawlspace. Edening buildings had crawlspaces everywhere. Wealthy grief required hidden infrastructure: cables, coolant, scent lines, emergency drainage for immersion bays, backup power for family sessions that could not be interrupted simply because the city failed.

The security men entered.

"Ms. Aster," one called.

Sable pushed the access panel aside.

"This is not disciplinary."

That was how she knew it was.

She pulled herself into the crawlspace, scraping one knee badly enough to leave blood. The space smelled of dust, ozone, and apple fragrance from a nearby scent line. Below, a stall door opened.

"Ms. Aster."

She crawled.

Her wrist console was locked out of Edening systems but still held the private copies: sleeping child faces, Orchard 4881 crash logs, Rowan's patch output, donor message. She transferred them to the children's atlas she had taken from the hallway outside her mother's apartment.

Not literally. The atlas was paper.

But tucked into its back cover was an old school memory wafer Rowan had used for a class presentation about planets. Sable had found it in the pages while waiting at the elevator and had almost thrown it away. Now she slid it into her console and copied everything under the file name PLUTO_RESTORATION.

Rowan would have approved.

At the end of the crawlspace, a grate looked down into the public memorial lobby.

Families moved below, unaware that an engineer with blood on one knee and stolen sky data was breathing above them. Edening's lobby was built around a living olive tree shipped from somewhere old enough to impress donors. Around it, families waited for visitation appointments. A boy in a suit held a bouquet. A woman practiced smiling before entering her dead father's orchard. A man argued softly with a concierge about whether his husband's continuity person could be aged down "just three years, not vanity, only health."

Sable almost hated them.

Then could not. That was the inconvenience on which Edening depended. The clients were ridiculous and grieving and sometimes monstrous. They also missed people. Missing made them vulnerable to anyone selling a room where the missing stopped moving away.

Security crossed the lobby below.

Sable kicked the grate loose.

It fell into the olive tree with a crash that made every family look up.

Sable dropped after it, landed badly in decorative mulch, and stood with as much dignity as mulch allowed.

The lobby froze.

The boy with the bouquet said, "Are you dead?"

"Not yet."

She ran.

Behind her, security finally abandoned elegance.

At the revolving doors, a woman stepped into their path. Rina Cho. She had no reason to be there, and every reason. She held Mae's contract folder against her chest.

"This way," Rina said.

Sable followed her into a waiting car driven by Tessa, who looked furious enough to qualify as weather.

As the car pulled into traffic, Sable looked back at Edening's glass facade. Families still stood in the lobby under the olive tree, staring at the hole she had fallen through.

For the first time, Edening's hidden infrastructure had dropped into public grief.

It would not be the last.


# Chapter 05: Sleep Clinic With No Windows

The children's sleep clinic had no windows, but every ceiling tile showed a sky.

That was the first lie. Not the worst, not even close, but the first. Sable stood in the reception area of the Halcyon Pediatric Sleep Institute and watched rendered clouds move across acoustic panels while a woman behind the desk explained that natural light interfered with neuro-restorative continuity studies. The clouds were too slow, too clean, moving with the bland confidence of weather that had never ruined anyone's plans.

On the wall behind reception, three logos overlapped in a polished civic braid:

HALCYON PEDIATRIC SLEEP INSTITUTE

MORROW FOUNDATION FOR CHILDHOOD REST

EDENING FAMILY CONTINUITY PARTNERS

The Edening logo was smallest. A leaf inside a circle. Almost shy.

"You said you were here for archival compliance?" the receptionist asked.

Sable had said no such thing. She had said "sky integration review," which apparently meant whatever the person hearing it feared most. She let the mistake live.

"Horizon irregularity," Sable said.

The receptionist's smile tightened. "We don't maintain horizon systems onsite."

"No one does, officially."

The woman looked down at Sable's badge, up at Sable's face, and made the calculation people made around corporate authority: whether refusing entry would create more paperwork than allowing it. Paperwork won.

"Dr. Pell is in session."

"I'll wait."

"Sessions run ninety minutes."

"I'm patient in windowless rooms. Childhood training."

The receptionist did not laugh.

Halcyon smelled of lavender disinfectant and warm plastic. Down the hall, painted doors carried names of planets: Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter. Children passed in socks, accompanied by parents holding tote bags and the peculiar guilt of adults who had consented to something they did not fully understand because a specialist had said regulation, development, opportunity, relief.

One boy of about seven stopped in front of Sable.

He stared at her shoes.

"You have orchard mud," he said.

Sable looked down. Her boots were clean. Edening immersion never left physical residue except in the mind, and sometimes hair scent, and sometimes guilt.

"Do I?"

He nodded. "Dead mud is shinier."

His mother tugged his hand. "Cai. Don't bother the lady."

"It's all right," Sable said.

The boy looked up then. His eyes were gray, heavy-lidded, too tired for his face. "Are you here to fix the star?"

The mother's grip tightened.

"What star?" Sable asked.

Cai's mouth opened.

"We have to go," his mother said.

She pulled him down the hall. The boy looked back once before disappearing through the door marked Jupiter.

The ceiling clouds continued their fraudulent drift.

Sable checked her wrist console. The illegal patch in her coat pocket was wrapped in signal foil, but even so it warmed whenever Halcyon's internal network pulsed. Lio's code had included a passive sniffer for sky-root calls. She had not asked for it. She hated that she was grateful.

Incoming signal:

DREAM DONOR SESSION / MINOR / HORIZON ELASTICITY CAPTURE

DESTINATION: EDENING SKY POOL / NURSERY_ATLAS_BRANCH_07

There it was. Not rumor. Not metaphor. Routing.

A nurse exited Mars with a tablet against her chest. Sable caught the door before it closed.

Inside was a sleep room arranged to look like a child's bedroom designed by a committee of people who had seen children through survey data. Soft rug. Moon lamp. Shelf of sanitized books. Bed with rails disguised as clouds. Above the bed, a projector cast stars onto the ceiling.

A girl slept under a weighted blanket.

Electrodes dotted her forehead. Her eyelids moved rapidly. Beside the bed, a machine translated her sleep into a live waveform labeled:

IMAGINATIVE DEPTH RESPONSE.

Not dream. Not child. Response.

Dr. Pell stood at the monitor. She was small, severe, maybe fifty, with hair shaved close to the scalp and a voice that arrived before her face turned.

"This is a restricted session."

"Sable Aster, Edening horizon integrity."

"I did not request Edening."

"No one requests horizon integrity until it fails."

Pell looked at Sable's badge, then at the door, then at the sleeping child. "Hallway."

Sable did not move. "What is the child's dream routed to?"

"You know I cannot discuss patient data."

"Is it patient data after you send it to a dead woman's sky?"

Pell's face changed. Not with guilt. With irritation at imprecision.

"You orchard engineers always dramatize infrastructure."

"You pediatric sleep doctors always rename extraction as care."

The girl's hand twitched under the blanket.

The star projector glitched. One star remained after the others flickered out, bright and low on the ceiling.

The wrong star.

Sable's wrist console vibrated hard enough to hurt.

Pell moved to the machine and entered a command. The star vanished. The child's breathing quickened.

"Don't suppress it," Sable said.

"You have no clinical authority here."

"And you have no orchard authority."

"This isn't an orchard."

The sleeping girl whispered, "Apple lady."

Sable went still.

Pell's fingers froze above the tablet.

"What did she say?"

"Normal verbal spillover," Pell said.

"She said apple lady."

"Children dream symbols."

"Orchard 4881 is an apple farm."

Pell closed the tablet case. "Out."

The girl whispered again, clearer this time.

"She doesn't like peas."

Elise Harrow, shelling peas in a heaven she hated.

Sable crossed to the bed.

Pell grabbed her arm. "You wake her abruptly, you could cause neural rebound."

"What is she carrying?"

"A compensated dream study."

"How old is she?"

"That is protected."

"What is her name?"

"Protected."

"Where do her dreams go?"

Pell said nothing.

They were not frightened at first. Only empty in the way people are empty when pulled from far away too quickly. Then she saw Sable and began to cry.

"I dropped the sky," she said.

Sable turned on Pell. "Turn that off."

"She's destabilizing."

"She's a child."

"She is a participant experiencing transitional distress."

Sable slapped the tablet from Pell's hand.

The chord stopped.

The girl gasped, then cried harder, which felt like a victory so awful Sable nearly bent under it.

The girl looked at Pell first.

"She can say it," Sable said.

"Ily," the girl whispered.

Ily Vale. The name hit Sable with the force of recognition before memory supplied why. Donor adolescents. Future revolt. The continuity checklist had not existed in-world, but the narrative pressure had. Here was a child who would not stay only a child.

"Ily," Sable said. "What sky did you drop?"

Ily wiped her face with the heel of her hand. "The one with the mean lady in the apple house."

Pell moved toward the door. Sable blocked her.

"Who pays for her participation?"

"Her guardian."

"Where is her guardian?"

"Consent and compensation records are managed centrally."

"Is she foster placement?"

Pell's silence answered.

Ily looked between them. "If I sleep good, my aunt gets rent."

There it was. The contract beneath the ceiling clouds.

Not a kidnapped child in chains. Not a villain's basement. Something cleaner. A caregiver behind on rent. A foundation offering compensation. A doctor saying harmless. A company needing sky.

Sable felt sick because the system had not hidden its cruelty in darkness. It had hidden it in everyone's desire to survive one more month.

Alarms began softly, politely, as if apologizing for caring.

Pell had triggered silent security.

Sable took the illegal patch from her coat and inserted it into the diagnostic port beside Ily's bed.

Pell's face went white. "What are you doing?"

"Asking where the sky goes."

The room changed.

The projected ceiling vanished. The acoustic tiles became transparent. Above the sleep room spread not pipes, not wires, but night: an enormous layered dark full of orchards from below. Sable saw Elise's apple farm, Martin's fake fireplace smoke, Rowan's lake edge, thousands of dead landscapes hanging upside down like fruit. Through all of them ran pale roots rising from sleeping rooms like this one.

Children's dreams braided into heaven.

Ily sat up in bed.

"Don't," she whispered.

"Don't what?"

"Look too long. They look back when you're awake."

Above them, one wrong star brightened.

Then another.

Then another.

Not stars. Knots. Places where a child's dream had been tied too tightly to the dead.

Security opened the door.

Sable ripped the patch free. The ceiling returned. The room became a room again, which was worse because now she knew what rooms could hide.

Pell pointed at Sable. "She is interfering with a clinical session."

The guard hesitated. Edening badge outranked clinic badge in ways no one admitted until doors had to choose.

Sable stepped close to Ily. "Do you remember the orchards when you're awake?"

Ily nodded.

"How many?"

"I don't count dead people. It makes them crowd."

"Do you know a boy named Rowan?"

Ily's eyes widened.

Pell said, "Enough."

Ily whispered, "He holds the old dark."

The guard took Sable's arm.

She let him, because fighting would leave Ily in the room with Pell and no witness. As he led her into the hall, Sable looked back.

Ily raised one hand. On her palm, in blue marker, she had drawn a star with roots.

The door closed.

In the reception area, the rendered clouds still moved without weather.

Sable walked out under their false sky carrying the knowledge that paradise had a pediatric wing.



# Chapter 06: The Contract Mothers

The first mother who sold her child's dreams brought receipts.

She met Sable in the basement of a laundromat three districts from Halcyon, beneath rows of machines that thumped and turned like mechanical hearts. Her name was Rina Cho. She was thirty-two, though exhaustion had filed years into the corners of her face. She wore a grocery uniform under a coat too thin for the rain and carried a folder in a plastic bag to protect it from weather, shame, or both.

"I didn't sell them," Rina said before sitting. "That's their word when they want me dirty enough to ignore."

"What word do you use?"

"Lease."

It was worse, somehow. More accurate.

Lio Venn had arranged the meeting after Sable messaged him three threats and one offer. He had replied with a location, a price, and the sentence: If you want mothers to confess, bring cash and no moral perfume.

Sable brought cash.

PARTICIPANT WILL CONTRIBUTE NONINVASIVE DREAM-ADJACENT COGNITIVE ACTIVITY TO PEDIATRIC REST RESEARCH.

COMPENSATION WILL SUPPORT HOUSEHOLD STABILITY.

NO PERSONHOOD, MEMORY, OR MEDICAL AUTONOMY WILL BE TRANSFERRED.

Sable read the last line twice.

"They paid you monthly?"

"If she completed enough sessions."

"She?"

"My daughter. Mae."

The name lay on the table between washers and receipts.

"How old?"

"Six when it started. Nine now."

Sable closed her eyes.

"Don't do that," Rina said.

"What?"

"Look like you're feeling my life for the first time."

Sable opened her eyes. "Fair."

Rina leaned back. "They said she had unusual dream resilience. They said the study helped children process fear. Mae used to have night terrors. After Halcyon, she slept."

"Did she dream?"

Rina's mouth tightened. "Not at first."

"And later?"

"Later she stopped drawing."

The laundromat's machines churned above them. Water behind glass. Clothes collapsing and rising.

"She used to draw cities with animals in the windows," Rina said. "Birds driving buses. Tigers buying bread. Stupid things. Good things. After a few months, she drew houses with no sky. Then boxes. Then she said dreams were for people who could afford to waste sleep."

Sable wrote nothing down.

"Did you stop?"

# Chapter 10: Dr. Morrow's Mercy

Dr. Vale Morrow invited Sable to breakfast in an orchard he claimed was not an orchard.

You have seen enough fragments to become cruel with them.

That was how Sable knew she would go.

Sable had seen it only in company history reels: greenhouses, old brick, founders smiling beside seed trays. # Chapter 11: Wrong-Star Weather

The first orchard to snow upward belonged to a child.

Edening called him Continuity Minor 72 because the family had sealed his name after the accident and the municipal grant contract had reduced him to protected classification. He had died at seven, three years before Sable joined the company, and lived now in a perpetual backyard summer behind a house his family had lost to foreclosure. His orchard was charity-tier but beloved by staff because the boy had low boundary curiosity and mostly used his continuation to build elaborate mud cities no family member could afford to visit often.

At 4:22 p.m., snow began rising from the grass into the sky.

Sable entered with Anik, who had stopped pretending she was not under investigation and started pretending accompanying her was supervision. The orchard smelled like hot grass, wet dirt, and cold.

The boy stood barefoot beside a mud city, looking upward.

Snowflakes lifted from the lawn in slow white drifts, passing through the leaves of a maple tree, disappearing into a wrong star bright enough to be visible in full day. The flakes did not melt. The boy held out both hands, not frightened yet. Curious.

"Is this a new weather?" he asked.

Anik whispered, "We should pull him."

"To where?" Sable said.

Continuity persons could be suspended into blank safety environments, but minors reacted badly. Blankness taught them faster than boundaries did.

Sable crouched beside the boy. "What's your name?"

He looked at Anik.

"You can tell me if you want," Sable said.

"Oren."

The orchard accepted the name. The maple leaves shimmered.

"Oren, did the snow say anything?"

"Snow doesn't talk."

"Good point."

"The star does, though."

Anik closed his eyes.

"What does it say?"

Oren picked up a mud twig and stirred one of his tiny streets. "It says the girl holding my sky is tired."

Sable looked up. Snow rose into the star like souls in a bad painting. Edening's comfort layer kept trying to convert the event into wonder. Soft music threatened in the air. She killed it.

Before she could ask another question, Anik's console opened three more feeds.

In a coastal orchard, a dead fisherman stood knee-deep in low tide while rain fell upward from the sand and collected in a dark cloud shaped like a crib. He kept removing his cap and putting it back on because the gesture was the only loop that still made sense to him.

In a honeymoon orchard, roses opened with children's baby teeth where petals should have been. The continuity bride touched one and screamed her husband's living name though he was not logged in.

In a charity memorial room, a dead woman woke to the sound of multiplication tables whispered from the walls. "I never learned them," she told an empty chair. "Who is studying in my house?"

The wrong-star weather was not random. It was biography leaking through stolen function. Children's fears, chores, schoolwork, missing teeth, asthma breath, carrot hatred, foster-office dread, all of it returning as weather to the dead who had mistaken it for horizon.

Anik muted the feeds one by one.

"Don't," Sable said.

"They destabilize residents."

"They identify victims."

"Both can be true."

"Then stop choosing only one."

"Do you know the girl's name?"

"Mae. But she doesn't like when dead people count her."

Mae Cho. Rina's daughter.

Sable stood too fast.

The mud city cracked.

Oren frowned. "Careful."

"I'm sorry."

He knelt and repaired the crack with two fingers. The gesture was so ordinary, so childishly serious, that Sable had to look away. This was what made Edening unbearable. The boy was not alive. He was not simply dead. He cared whether a mud road held.

Anik's console flashed.

WEATHER INVERSION SPREADING.

Across Edening, orchards began reporting wrong-star weather: rain falling as lullabies, wind carrying unfamiliar bedtime stories, fruit ripening with children's teeth marks, night arriving in rooms scheduled for morning, dead residents waking from dreams of school buses and foster forms. The Atlas was no longer hidden horizon. It was leaking content.

Oren looked at Sable. "Is Mae real?"

"Yes."

"More real than me?"

Anik inhaled.

There were whole legal teams built to avoid that question.

"Different real," Sable said.

Oren considered this. "Does different real mean I can't help her?"

Sable felt the question strike the entire system.

"I don't know."

"Adults say that before no."

"Sometimes before yes."

He looked at the rising snow. "If I give back some sky, will my yard get smaller?"

Anik said, "We can't ask him that."

Oren turned. "I'm right here."

Anik looked ashamed. "I know."

"No," the boy said. "You don't."

The orchard went silent.

For a moment, Oren looked older than seven. Not because his model aged, but because being treated as absent while present had taught him a kind of age Edening did not measure.

Sable opened Morrow's Winter Patch drive.

Anik grabbed her wrist. "Not in a minor orchard."

"He asked."

"He is a continuity minor."

"He is right here."

The words were Oren's. They worked differently in Sable's mouth. Not as innocence. As indictment.

She loaded a limited finite-weather prototype into a sandbox branch and explained it to Oren as plainly as she could.

"Your yard may become smaller. The sky may stop pretending to go forever. You may notice the edge. But it might reduce how much Mae has to carry."

Oren listened. Then asked, "Will my mom still visit?"

"Yes."

"Will she be sad my yard is smaller?"

"Probably."

"She cries when the mud city changes."

"Yes."

He looked at the cracked road he had repaired.

"Make it winter," he said.

Anik opened a private channel. "His mother has an anniversary visit tomorrow."

Sable looked at him.

"She pays with grant credits and one overtime cleaning shift a month," he said. "She asks for summer because she says he died in winter and she cannot see him cold."

Oren pretended not to listen. The mud twig snapped in his fingers.

"Does she decide the weather?" he asked.

Anik closed his eyes.

Sable answered. "She has been allowed to."

"Because she's alive?"

"Because Edening thought family grief was safer to sell than your preference was to ask."

Oren considered that with grave disgust. "Adults make very long mistakes."

"Yes."

"Then make it winter before she comes. So she knows I know."

Anik stepped back.

Sable initiated the patch.

The snow stopped rising.

For three seconds nothing happened. Then the far fence appeared.

It had always been there, hidden beyond comfort depth, but now it rendered plainly: a wooden fence at the edge of the yard. Beyond it, not endless summer, not fake neighborhood, not horizon borrowed from Mae, but a soft gray blank. Honest absence.

Oren stared.

The mud city trembled. The maple tree lost leaves. Summer cooled into late autumn. The sky shrank.

Oren began to cry.

Sable nearly reversed the patch.

Anik's hand hovered near override.

But the wrong star dimmed, and somewhere above or beneath the orchard, a living child exhaled in sleep.

Oren wiped his face with muddy hands. "I hate it."

"I know."

"It feels true."

"I know."

"I hate that too."

He walked to the fence and touched it. His small hand rested on the boundary. The wood did not pretend to continue.

"Can Mae have the snow back?" he asked.

Sable checked the Atlas draw. Mae Cho's branch load had reduced by three percent.

Three percent. A child's yard made smaller, and a living girl's sleep relieved by three percent.

The arithmetic of hell had acquired decimals.

"Some," Sable said.

Oren nodded. "Then don't make it summer when my mom comes."

"She may ask."

"Tell her I said dead boys can be cold."

Sable logged the preference.

For the first time in Edening history, a continuity minor had knowingly accepted boundary reduction to relieve a dream donor.

The system categorized it as:

ANOMALOUS SELF-HARM EVENT.

Sable deleted the tag and wrote:

CONSENT.


# Chapter 12: The Dream Donor Revolt

The donors did not revolt by waking.

They had been awake for years. They went to school, fought with guardians, failed math, kissed people badly, took buses, scrolled feeds, made toast, forgot homework, learned to drive, stole eyeliner, became taller than the adults who had signed for them. The revolt began when they refused to sleep on schedule.

At first it looked like adolescent contagion. Halcyon reported noncompliance across active cohorts. Sleep clinics blamed social media, stimulant drinks, exam stress, the usual adult pantheon of causes that made children responsible for symptoms. Then Edening's sky pool dropped twelve percent in one night.

Orchards all over the network lost distance.

Families called.

The dead looked up.

Sable found the donors in an abandoned shopping mall where the skylights had been painted black from the inside.

Ily sent the address through Elise's orchard. Not by message exactly. Elise handed Sable an apple in which the seeds arranged themselves into coordinates, then said, "Children are better at drama than secrecy." The coordinates led to the mall's third floor, past shuttered stores and a carousel with no animals, into a former mattress showroom filled with teenagers trying not to fall asleep.

There were twenty-seven of them.

Some no older than twelve. Some nearly adults. They sat under harsh portable lights, drinking coffee, slapping one another awake, playing loud music with no rhythm because rhythm invited dreams. On the wall behind them, someone had painted the star-with-roots symbol in black.

Ily Vale stood at the center wearing an oversized jacket and an expression too tired for revolution.

"You came," she said.

"You asked."

"Adults usually treat that as decoration."

Sable looked around. "How long have you been awake?"

"Depends who. Cai's on thirty-six hours. Mae's on twenty-two. I'm on forty."

"You could hurt yourselves."

A boy laughed from a pile of blankets. "We noticed."

Ily crossed her arms. "Sleeping hurts us too."

Sable had no good answer because the truth had trapped them between injuries.

Lio emerged from behind a stack of rolled mattresses, carrying a box of battery lamps.

"Don't look at me like that," he said. "I brought lighting and morally neutral crackers."

"You knew?"

"They found me first. Apparently I have a brand among exploited sky youth."

"He sells broken heavens," Ily said. "We thought he might buy a broken sky."

Lio put a hand to his chest. "My reputation, weaponized."

Sable turned back to Ily. "What do you want?"

The room changed. Not because Ily moved, but because every donor became attentive. The question had entered like weather.

"We want our dreams back," Ily said.

"I don't know if that's possible."

"We know."

"We want Edening to say what it did," said Mae Cho, small, hair braided with red string. "With our names if we choose. Not donor cohorts."

"We want guardians paid without making us sleep for dead people," said Nico, Tessa's nephew.

"We want the dead told," said Cai, the boy from Halcyon reception.

That surprised Sable.

"Told what?"

"That we're real," he said. "Some of them think we're weather."

A girl near the lights whispered, "Some know."

The room went quiet.

Sable looked at her. "What do you mean?"

The girl was older, seventeen maybe, with cropped hair and silver crescents under both eyes drawn in marker to keep herself awake. "I was in a Grove-tier sky. Rich dead man. He knew I was dreaming him mountains. He said thank you and asked if I could make them taller."

No one moved.

There it was. The next moral rot. Not hidden exploitation. Conscious preference.

"Name?" Sable asked.

"His or mine?"

"Both if you want."

"Mine is Jessa. His can stay hungry."

Ily touched Jessa's shoulder.

Sable thought of Morrow: If they are persons, their terror matters. Yes. And if they were persons, their choices could become crimes.

"Not all continuity persons know," Sable said.

Ily's eyes narrowed. "Are you defending them?"

"I'm refusing to simplify them."

"That is what adults call defending when they want time."

"Sometimes."

"Do we have time?"

Sable looked at the children fighting sleep under blacked-out skylights. "No."

She told them about Oren accepting the Winter Patch. About Elise burying her false sky. About Rowan under the Atlas. She did not spare herself. She told them she had maintained orchards, adjusted weather, hidden boundaries, believed company language long after suspicion should have become refusal.

When she said Rowan's name, the donors shifted.

Ily looked down. "We know him."

"How?"

"He keeps the old dark from swallowing the first branches. Sometimes when we sleep, he takes the worst part."

Sable felt anger and gratitude collide so violently she had to sit on a rolled mattress.

"He shouldn't have to."

"Neither should we," Ily said.

"No."

The answer mattered. The room heard it.

Lio handed Sable a lamp and lowered his voice. "Edening security is sweeping donor forums. They will find this place."

"How long?"

"Hours. Maybe less if one of them has finally hired a teenager."

Ily stepped forward. "We want to strike properly."

"Meaning?"

"Sleep together."

Sable stared.

"If we all enter at once and refuse horizon load, the wrong star spreads. Every orchard sees."

"You could overload."

"We're already loaded."

"You don't know what refusal does in the Atlas."

"Neither did Rowan."

That silenced her.

Cai spoke from the blankets. "We need an adult to open the route and not own it."

Sable looked around the room. "Why me?"

Ily's expression softened for the first time. Not kindness. Recognition.

"Because the boy under the sky gave you the star."

Sable touched her sternum. The pressure there had not left since Atlas. Rowan's refusal, lodged like a second heart.

"And because," Ily added, "you look guilty enough to maybe listen."

Lio coughed. "High praise."

Sable stood.

"If we do this, we do not use anyone's dream without consent. Anyone can leave. Anyone can sleep or stay awake. No one becomes symbol by accident."

Jessa laughed. "You write policies when scared."

"Yes."

"Fine. Write this one: we are not weather."

Sable wrote it on the wall under the star-with-roots.

WE ARE NOT WEATHER.

The donors cheered softly because loud joy wasted energy.

Then they argued for forty minutes about rules.

Not noble arguments. Practical ones. Whether guardians could wake them if a donor changed their mind while asleep. Whether a thirteen-year-old in state care could consent without the state that had sold him. Whether Ily counted as coordinator or just loudest exhausted person in the room. Whether Sable's promise to open a route made her different from Halcyon or only later in the chain.

"No adult owns the switch," Mae said.

"Someone has to," Lio said. "Switches are tragically binary."

"Then three switches," Cai said from the blankets. "Mine, hers, and the adult who doesn't want it."

They built that. It was ugly. Three physical cutoffs for each donor: one in the donor's hand, one with a chosen peer or guardian, one on Sable's board. Any one could sever the route. The system would be unstable. Lio complained about topology. Ily told him topology could suffer character growth.

Rina arrived after midnight with a backpack full of oranges, inhalers, clean socks, and the expression of a mother who had signed a child's sleep away and now had to ask permission to sit near the mattress.

Mae saw her and went still.

"You can't decide for me," Mae said.

Rina put the backpack down. "I know."

"You didn't know before."

"No."

"If I say don't touch the switch, don't."

Rina's face broke around the instruction and held. "I won't."

Mae looked at Sable. "Write it."

Sable wrote it.

Rina sat three feet from her daughter's mattress and did not move closer.

By midnight, they had arranged mattresses in a circle. Battery lamps burned around them. Lio built a route through black-market sky patches. Sable connected Morrow's Winter Patch drive, Elise's buried-sky logs, Oren's consent marker, and Rowan's star. It was not a clean system. It was a room full of harmed children preparing to enter the place that harmed them because no adult had built a safer door in time.

Before they slept, Ily took Sable's hand.

"If we don't wake up?"

Sable wanted to promise. Did not.

"Then I come in after you."

"That's also not a promise."

"It's what I have."

Ily nodded. "Better."

One by one, the donors lay down.

For the first time in days, the room went quiet.

Above them, under the black-painted skylights, the wrong star appeared without a projector.

It pulsed once.

Then the children slept.


# Chapter 13: Family Access Riot

Edening opened the family atrium at six in the morning because grief, properly monetized, kept office hours.

By seven, the atrium was full.

Not full in the marketing sense, not the tasteful gatherings Sable had seen on launch days when investors drifted beneath living citrus walls and asked whether eternity could be made quarterly. Above the reception desk, Edening's public-facing wall still played the standard loop. A woman in a sunlit vineyard laughed with a continuity version of her father. FAMILY COMMUNICATION PROTOCOL REVISION.

# Chapter 21: The Orchard Of Wrong Stars

By the time Sable entered the collapsing Atlas, the stars were falling upward.

Toward Rowan.

# Chapter 22: What The Dead Keep

The dead did not vanish all at once.

# Chapter 23: Real Sky Garden

The witness garden opened on land no one wanted.

It had been a municipal parking lot behind a shuttered funeral home, wedged between an elevated train line and a drainage canal. Edening owned better land and offered it twice, first through apology and then through tax strategy. Sable refused both. If the new place began as a gift from the old heaven company, it would spend its life tasting like citrus and settlement.

The donors chose the lot.

"Ugly sky," Ily said, looking up through train tracks.

"Real," Mae said.

That settled it.

They tore up asphalt in winter. Not metaphorically. With rented equipment, volunteers, ex-Edening engineers, angry parents, bored teenagers, three continuity-family widowers, two nurses, one judge, Lio on supervised release, and Martin Harrow, who arrived in expensive boots and ruined them without complaint. Dr. Morrow petitioned to contribute design notes from custody. Sable read them, used one irrigation suggestion, and burned the rest in a coffee can because not every useful thing deserved preservation.

They planted trees that would take years to matter.

Apple, pear, fig, serviceberry, hawthorn. Nothing seedless. The donors insisted on that. Fruit with futures or no fruit at all.

The first argument was about benches.

Martin Harrow wanted memorial benches. He had catalogues, tasteful plaques, wood certified by organizations with leaves in their logos. Rina Cho said benches turned guilt into furniture. Tessa said people still needed to sit. Jessa said if anyone put her name on a bench she would set it on fire, then clarified she meant legally, then refused to clarify further. Lio suggested movable chairs, ugly ones, the kind nobody could mistake for tribute. Mae voted for chairs because houses had chairs and benches belonged to parks where adults watched you.

They bought forty mismatched folding chairs from a closed church.

The second argument was about whether continuity persons could donate labor. Alma Ret wanted to design a children's reading corner. Sibyl Auer wanted to fund a piano, badly tuned by design. Oren wanted to send mud recipes, which made no sense and later became the garden's most popular workshop. Donor advocates worried the dead would make themselves sympathetic and repossess the story. Continuity advocates argued that refusing all contribution turned finite persons into exhibits of harm rather than members of a damaged public.

Sable wrote the rule after three meetings and one walk along the drainage canal.

The dead may offer. The living harmed may refuse. No offer purchases forgiveness.

Everyone disliked it for different reasons. That was how Sable knew it was close.

The garden's central structure was not an interface suite. It was a weather shelter with a retractable roof. Visits, when they happened, happened under the actual sky. Continuity persons could be brought into limited finite presence for short intervals only if they had logged preference, family consent did not override their refusal, and no donor dream material was used. The system rendered no horizon. If a dead person looked beyond the garden, they saw the same train tracks and canal as everyone else.

Most families hated the first visits.

"It's so small," a woman said after meeting her dead brother under the shelter.

Her brother, a continuity person who had lost a Grove-tier hunting preserve and chosen not to dissolve, looked at the canal and said, "So was our apartment."

They sat for eleven minutes. He complained about the smell. She cried because complaining was the first thing that sounded like him.

The second visit failed.

A family brought a dead mother who had chosen finite presence under court pressure but not garden presence. Her sons insisted she would be grateful once she arrived. Edening's old culture had trained families to treat the dead's preferences as customer inputs, adjustable when inconvenient. Sable refused the render at the gate.

"She logged no," Sable said.

The eldest son held up a folder thick with payment records. "We maintained her for eight years."

"You did."

"She was confused when she said no."

"She may have been."

"We are her family."

"Yes."

"Then who are you to stop us?"

Sable looked past him at the shelter, where rainwater collected in one corner because the roof seal still leaked.

"The stop," she said.

He called her cruel. His brother called her a murderer. Their wives looked embarrassed and relieved. They left with the folder and returned two weeks later without it, asking whether they could sit under the open roof without summoning anyone. Sable said yes. The garden had to allow grief that was not ready to behave.

Elise visited the garden on opening week.

She appeared in a black dress, older than Martin's preferred version because she had amended herself after winning a legal fight no one knew how to categorize. Martin came with her. He stood outside the shelter, alive and shivering. Elise looked up through the open roof at a clouded evening sky.

"Disappointing," she said.

"Yes," Sable replied.

"Good."

The dog could not come. Elise pretended not to mind and failed.

Oren refused garden visits for two months, then arrived with a box of mud rendered from his finite yard. He and Mae built a city together under the shelter while their mothers watched. Mae did not speak to him at first. Then she told him he made roads wrong. He said dead boys were allowed poor urban planning. She said not in her city. Their argument lasted forty minutes and made both mothers cry.

Sable tried not to watch too closely.

The garden had rules painted on a board near the entrance.

NO INFINITY.

NO VISIT WITHOUT CONSENT.

NO CHILD DREAMS.

NO FAMILY AMENDMENT OF THE DEAD WITHOUT THE DEAD.

NO PROMISE OF CLOSURE.

WEATHER IS WEATHER.

The last rule was Lio's. Sable had argued it was redundant. The donors overruled her.

Lawsuits continued. Edening fractured into divisions: finite continuity, memorial archives, litigation trusts, criminal investigations, underground restoration markets. Some families rebuilt illegal infinities using bootleg code and whatever dream residue remained in old caches. Some continuity persons begged for them. Some donors sold access as adults, claiming the right to monetize what had been taken. The world did not become clean because Sable broke one sky.

On the first night the garden opened to the public, rain came through the retractable roof before anyone remembered to close it.

People ran. Equipment got wet. A continuity man laughed so hard his render destabilized. Ily stood in the rain with her face tilted up, eyes closed.

Sable moved beside her.

"Roof?" she asked.

"No."

They stood until both were soaked.

"Do you dream now?" Sable asked.

Ily opened one eye. "Rude."

"Yes."

"Sometimes."

"Of what?"

"Small things. A cup. A dog I never had. Once a room with three doors and I didn't open any." She wiped rain from her face. "No sky yet."

"Maybe it takes time."

"Maybe I don't owe sky to anyone, including me."

Sable accepted that.

Across the garden, Mara Aster stood under a young apple tree, not entering the shelter, not approaching the continuity families. She came often and participated rarely. Some days she helped weed. Some days she sat by the canal and looked ruined by ordinary water.

Sable joined her.

"The tree looks bad," Mara said.

"It's winter."

"I know that. I am not completely decorative."

They watched rain bead on the young branches.

"Would he like this?" Mara asked.

Rowan. Always Rowan, even when unnamed.

Sable looked at the ugly lot, the real rain, the rules, the donors, the dead under a roof that leaked because no system was allowed to pretend weather would always behave.

"He'd say the trees were too organized."

Mara smiled. It hurt to see.

"He would," she said.

The train passed overhead, loud enough to end conversation. For once, no one softened the sound. The dead heard it. The living heard it. The garden shook.

When it passed, the sky remained.

Not wrong.

Not right.

Only there.

Later, after the public left, Sable found Lio tightening bolts on the weather shelter roof with the concentration of a man performing community service against his will and discovering competence was still available to him.

"Your roof leaks," he said.

"Our roof."

"Legally dangerous pronoun."

"You helped build it."

"I helped steal from heaven and then build a municipal shed. My career arc is hostile to summary."

Sable handed him a wrench.

For a while they worked without speaking. The garden at night looked worse than during the day. Young trees in cages. Mud paths. Folding chairs stacked under tarps. The canal giving off its chemical smell. No one would have mistaken it for paradise unless they had learned suspicion from beauty.

"Rowan would hate the shed," Lio said.

Sable tightened a bolt too hard. "You didn't know Rowan."

"No."

"Then don't."

He accepted that and worked three more bolts in silence.

At the gate, Ily waited under the real sky, not looking up. That was her habit now. Sable did not ask whether avoidance was healing or injury. Some things did not improve when named too early.

The garden lights clicked off one row at a time.

For a second the whole lot was dark enough to show three stars between train tracks.

Ily looked then.

Only then.


# Chapter 24: The Star That Was Not There

On the first clear night after winter, Sable and her mother went looking for a star they knew would not appear.

They drove to the lake where Rowan had drowned because grief, like weather, returned to low places. The road out of the city was lined with real orchards, bare-branched, unlit, owned by farmers who had no interest in metaphysics beyond frost. Mara slept in the passenger seat for twenty minutes with her mouth slightly open. Sable drove and tried not to find tenderness inconvenient.

They stopped once at a gas station outside a town famous for cherries and a courthouse that had recently banned Edening advertisements from municipal property.

Inside, a rack of cheap magazines displayed three versions of the scandal. One cover showed Morrow being led from Greenhouse 3 between two federal agents, his face turned away from cameras. Another showed a donor hospital window with the caption THE CHILDREN WHO HELD HEAVEN. The third showed a doctored image of a wrong star above a field of graves. Sable turned that one facedown.

Mara came up beside her holding two coffees and a packet of powdered donuts Rowan would have begged for and then abandoned after two bites.

"Does it help?" Mara asked, looking at the magazine rack.

"What?"

"Everyone knowing."

Sable considered lying because the truth was not efficient.

"It helps some things."

"Not him."

"No."

"Not us."

"Not enough."

Mara paid for the coffee. The clerk recognized Sable while handing over change. His eyes widened. He opened his mouth, perhaps to thank her, accuse her, ask for help with a dead aunt, demand an explanation of whether his father's subscription would survive the injunction. Sable prepared several inadequate answers.

He said, "Bathroom key's broken. Door just sticks."

"Thanks," Sable said.

Outside, Mara laughed until she had to lean against the car.

"What?" Sable asked.

"Door just sticks," Mara said.

Sable laughed too, unexpectedly and without permission from the dead.

For ten seconds they were two women in a gas station parking lot with bad coffee, and grief did not leave but failed to organize the moment.

Then they got back in the car.

The lake was smaller than memory and larger than forgiveness.

They parked by the public dock. No memorial stood there. Mara had refused one after the funeral, then regretted refusing, then regretted the regret because it had kept the dock more honest. Tonight the water was black and calm. Stars hung above it in their indifferent arrangements. No roots. No wrong light. No child holding them up.

Mara carried a thermos. Sable carried an outdated children's astronomy atlas.

They sat at the end of the dock with their feet above the water.

For a long time neither spoke.

Then Mara said, "I looked up Morrow's sentencing."

"Don't."

"I wanted to know if it helped."

"Did it?"

"No."

"Good."

Her mother sighed. "You have become difficult in ways I cannot take credit for."

"You can take some."

"Thank you. Horrifying."

The old rhythm appeared for one second and startled them both.

Mara opened the thermos. Coffee, too sweet. Rowan had liked sweet coffee before anyone allowed him coffee. He drank the foam from Mara's mug and declared bitterness a failure of imagination.

Sable took the atlas from her bag.

"I found this the day the reliquary failed."

Mara touched the cover. "He had one."

"I know."

"He was furious about Pluto."

"I know."

They opened it between them. The pages were worn, planets bright and wrong, facts outdated by newer measurements and older stubbornness. On the Pluto page, Sable had written in pencil:

RESTORED BY ORDER OF ROWAN ASTER.

Mara laughed.

Then cried.

There was no transition. No dignity. She bent over the book and cried like someone whose dead child had not become a star, had not returned, had not been redeemed by the exposure of a corporation or the building of a garden or the legal recognition of harms too elaborate for apology.

Sable put one arm around her.

Her mother leaned into it.

"I thought if he was useful," Mara said into Sable's coat, "then losing him had a shape."

"I know."

"I let them make him useful."

"Yes."

"I loved him."

"Yes."

Mara cried harder because the yeses did not fight. They only stood beside each other, irreconcilable and true.

Above the lake, the real stars did nothing.

Sable had expected to hate them for that. Instead she found their indifference almost merciful. No message. No system. No diagnostic. No child behind the light. If Rowan was anywhere, he was not available through the sky. If he was nowhere, the sky did not lie about that either.

Her mother laughed weakly.

That had to be enough.

No.

That had to be what there was.
